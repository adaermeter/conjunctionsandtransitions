What are Paired Conjunctions?

Paired conjunctions (also called correlative conjunctions) work in pairs to connect words, phrases, or clauses. They must maintain parallel structure in the sentence.

üí° Key Rule: Parallel Structure

Both parts of the paired conjunction must be followed by the same grammatical structure (both nouns, both verbs, both adjectives, etc.).

Correct: She likes both swimming and hiking. (both + gerund)

Both...and

Both X and Y

Connects two positive ideas of equal importance. Used to emphasize that two things are true.

Examples:

‚Ä¢ Both my sister and my brother are doctors.

‚Ä¢ She is both intelligent and hardworking.

‚Ä¢ He both sings and dances professionally.

‚Ä¢ They visited both Paris and London.

üéØ Usage Notes:

‚Ä¢ Use with plural verbs when connecting subjects: "Both John and Mary are coming."

‚Ä¢ Can connect any parallel structures: nouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, etc.

Either...or

Either X or Y

Presents a choice between two alternatives. Indicates that one of two possibilities will happen.

Examples:

‚Ä¢ You can have either coffee or tea.

‚Ä¢ Either study hard or fail the exam.

‚Ä¢ We can meet either today or tomorrow.

‚Ä¢ She will either call or text you.

üéØ Verb Agreement:

‚Ä¢ The verb agrees with the closer subject: "Either you or I am wrong."

‚Ä¢ In formal writing, some prefer: "Either you or I is wrong."

Neither...nor

Neither X nor Y

Connects two negative ideas. Means "not X and not Y".

Examples:

‚Ä¢ Neither John nor Mary came to the party.

‚Ä¢ She neither called nor texted me.

‚Ä¢ The food was neither hot nor fresh.

‚Ä¢ He neither smokes nor drinks.

üéØ Important Rules:

‚Ä¢ Use with positive verbs (no double negatives): "She neither likes coffee nor tea."

‚Ä¢ Verb agreement follows the same rule as "either...or".

Not only...but also

Not only X but also Y

Emphasizes the second element while including the first. Often used to show surprise or add important information.

Examples:

‚Ä¢ She is not only beautiful but also intelligent.

‚Ä¢ He not only sings but also dances well.

‚Ä¢ They not only finished the project but also exceeded expectations.

‚Ä¢ The car is not only fast but also fuel-efficient.

üéØ Inversion Rule:

When "not only" starts a sentence, use inversion: "Not only does she speak English, but she also speaks French."

Whether...or

Whether X or Y

Shows alternatives or uncertainty between possibilities.

Examples:

‚Ä¢ I don't know whether to laugh or cry.

‚Ä¢ Whether you like it or not, we have to finish this.

‚Ä¢ She'll come whether it rains or shines.

Academic & Formal Writing
